For the First Time in 43 Years, No Cross-talk! Only 6 Language Programs Left in 2026 Spring Festival Gala

LI Jiawei YANG Han YU Ziqi

The 2026 Spring Festival Gala boasts impressive data: 23.063 billion reaches, and its viewership share hits a 13-year high. However, the language programs, traditionally a highlight of the Gala, face severe challenges. Their number has sharply decreased to 6, and for the first time ever, there is no cross-talk (xiangsheng) program. This article briefly analyzes the historical changes and creative dilemmas behind this phenomenon.

Historical Comparison: A Cliff-Like Decline in the Number of Language Programs

Since the first Spring Festival Gala in 1983, language programs have always been a focal point for audiences. However, from the initial 17 programs to 6 in 2026, the number has plummeted by 64.7%. More notably, the 2026 Gala has no cross-talk program for the first time, marking a significant change in the ecosystem of language performances. This trend reflects creators' adjustments under multiple pressures, including program innovation, changing audience tastes, and censorship.

Among the only 6 language programs, the familiar traditional skits account for only half, while cross-talk has been replaced by two emerging forms: 2 "comedy sketches" resembling variety show scenarios ("You'll Like It", "Blood Pressure Monitor"), and 1 innovative form "dialogical vernacular" ("Whose Dish Is It?") that blends dialect with fast-paced dialogue. The reduction in the duration of language programs and their fragmentation means they can no longer serve as the backbone of the Gala. Meanwhile, a large number of technology showcases and song-and-dance performances dominate this year's Gala, accounting for approximately 64%. It is evident that the Spring Festival Gala is gradually shifting towards a "short, fast, and direct" model, pursuing audiovisual impact and immediate emotional feedback.

Creative Dilemma: Content is King vs. Safety First

"Every year, skits and cross-talk are the main targets of criticism. This shows that audiences have higher expectations for language programs, but creators also face immense pressure."

—— Zhang Yiwu (Cultural Scholar)

"It's not that we don't want to write good pieces, it's that we really can't produce anything better. There are too many restrictions on what can be said and performed now."

—— Yue Yunpeng (Cross-talk Performer)

"An overly stringent censorship mechanism can stifle creators' vitality, leading everyone to 'play it safe' to avoid mistakes."

—— Feng Xiaogang (Director, Screenwriter)
